# What a Plant Knows: A Field Guide to the Senses (Chapter 1), by Daniel Chamovitz

The experiment Darwin and his son did was very interesting, especially with how there were 5 plants but altered in different ways. This is a good example on how to conduct an experiment in order to make useful findings. Also, the part about farmers making it seem like the day was actually longer than it was to the plant using artificial light was very interesting. Not even plants are safe from manipulation. It's a cold world

# The Incredible Experience of Being Where You Are (Chapter 9), by Richard Louv

The idea of 'place blindness' is interesting since it's not just about being unfamiliar with somewhere, and can be a form of avoidance. San Diego had much more biodiversity than the author had thought for years. I haven't heard of live fences before, but after looking them up, I see that they are really cool. I thought they would just be wooden fences with vines growing on them, but there can be trees, branches, and bushes serving as the fence itself.

# The history of public participation in ecological research, by A Miller-Rushing et al.

The tracking of cherry blossom festivals for 1200 years was not intended to contribute to science, but it did many years later. This is a form of citizen science, and before science got professionalized most people doing science were amateurs. Professionalization shrank that role, but citizen science has kept science connected to places that professional researchers would not bother with.
